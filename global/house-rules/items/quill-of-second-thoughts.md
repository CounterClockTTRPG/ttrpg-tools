# Quill of Second Thoughts (Homebrew Item)

## Description

A single black feather quill, slightly longer than a man's hand, with an iridescent sheen that shifts between deep indigo and flat crow-black depending on the light. It holds ink normally and writes normally. The uncanny property announces itself only after the first stroke: whatever the wielder writes, a faint second line appears directly beneath in pale pencil-grey — the words the writer *actually meant*, as distinct from what they chose to put on the page.

The grey text fades within roughly a minute, leaving only the written words behind. No one has determined how the quill knows the difference.

The quill was, according to a merchant who sold one to a wizard who later sold it to someone else, originally made for a notary of unusual conscience. Most notaries who have since encountered it consider it professionally catastrophic.

## Mechanics

- **Appearance.** A plain, slightly oversized black feather quill. No visible markings. Detect Magic reveals an aura; identify or *legend lore* names the property.
- **The second line.** Whenever the wielder writes anything with the quill, the words they genuinely intended appear faintly beneath the written text in pencil-grey ink. Duration: approximately 1 minute (10 rounds) from the moment of writing, then fade completely. Only creatures who can read the written language see the grey text; the script is in the same tongue as what was written.
- **Scope.** The property reflects *intent*, not deeper subconscious truth. It shows what the writer meant to communicate but chose not to write — deliberate omission, diplomatic softening, tactical deception. It does not reveal hidden motivations, unconscious beliefs, or anything the writer was not actively thinking about while writing. A character who genuinely believes what they are writing produces no second line.
- **Diplomatic use.** Any letter, contract, treaty, or formal document produced with this quill while a recipient (or their agent) is present risks exposure. If the writer deceives while writing, the reader may notice the grey text: the reader makes a Wisdom check (DC 12, modified by circumstances — rushed reading, poor light, and distance all apply penalties). On a success, they read both lines.
- **Tactical use.** Codes and ciphers are unaffected — the quill shows intended meaning, not the key to a substitution cipher. A letter that says "the cargo arrives Thursday" where Thursday means *attack at dawn* shows no second line, because the surface text and the intended message are the same from the writer's perspective: the cipher *is* the message.
- **Voluntary suppression.** A writer who knows the quill's property and consciously crafts prose where every written word exactly matches their intent produces no second line — this requires effort and awareness but is not a roll. Practiced users (thieves, diplomats, lawyers) who know the quill adapt within a few minutes of use. The difficulty is that the quill never announces when someone *else* is watching.
- **Limitations.** The quill needs ink; it is not a stylus. It functions normally as a writing instrument in all other respects. It cannot be used as a weapon (too fragile). It does not affect spoken words, carved runes, or anything written by someone other than the current holder.

## Rarity and Value

Uncommon. Worth 200–400 gp to a scholar, archivist, or inquisitor; worth considerably less to anyone who has tried to use it for correspondence. Merchants tend to resell it quickly.

## DM Notes

The quill's most interesting use is social pressure, not mechanical advantage. The player knows it might expose them; NPCs who know it will watch carefully; NPCs who don't know it may notice something odd and ask. The real effect is that writing anything with it becomes a small performance — even when the writer is being honest.

For adjudicating the grey text in play: ask the player what their character *meant to say* versus what they actually wrote, then decide whether those differ. If there is no gap, there is no grey text. The Wisdom check applies only to active deception, not ambiguity.

# Marten Martialist's Shrinking Blade (Homebrew Item)

## Description

At rest it is a heavy bronze two-handed sword, plain and green-tarnished, with no markings to suggest it is anything more than an old battlefield relic. Those who carry it long enough learn otherwise. With each life it takes, the blade waits for its next drawing and then *changes* — shortening, brightening, hardening through a cycle of metals until what began as a clumsy two-hander ends its days as a slim dagger of gold alloy that cuts like a war-sword.

The story attached to it credits one Marten the Martialist, a duellist who is said to have carried it from bronze to gold across a lifetime of killings and died, finally, as an old man with a golden knife at his belt. Whatever the truth, only one such blade is known, and scholars who have examined it agree it is a minor artifact rather than a craftable weapon — it cannot be reproduced, and it does not behave as ordinary enchantment does.

## Mechanics

- **Appearance.** A weapon of shifting form. *Detect Magic* reveals a strong, oddly layered aura; *identify* names only its current bonus, while *legend lore* reveals the full five-stage cycle and the reversion clause. Its true nature resists casual analysis as befits a minor artifact.
- **The cycle.** The blade advances one stage after each *killing blow*, taking effect the next time it is drawn: (1) bronze two-handed sword, +1; (2) iron bastard sword, +2; (3) steel longsword, +3; (4) silver-alloy short sword, +4; (5) gold-alloy dagger, +5. Each form deals the normal damage of that weapon type at its listed bonus. Once it reaches the gold dagger, it remains a +5 dagger for the rest of that possessor's life.
- **The dagger's gift.** In its final form the +5 gold dagger deals damage *as a two-handed sword* (1d10 / 3d6 against L), not as a dagger. This is its most prized property.
- **Reversion.** If the blade is lost, stolen, or freely gifted, or if its owner dies, it reverts to the bronze two-handed sword (+1) and the cycle begins anew for the next wielder. If the possessor is merely *separated* from it without surrendering ownership, it holds its current form until physically claimed by another.
- **Adamantine for saves.** In any item saving-throw situation the blade is always treated as magical adamantine, regardless of its current metal — except against magnetic force, to which the iron and steel forms are vulnerable as their mundane metal.
- **Limitations.** It sheds no light and has no other powers. If thrown, it loses its magical bonus and strikes as a mundane weapon of its present form (the gold dagger thus deals only 1d4) — but the hit still counts as a magical weapon for what it can harm.

## Rarity and Value

Very rare — unique, and properly a minor artifact rather than market goods. No honest price applies, but a collector who understood it might offer 25,000 gp or more; a working warrior would value the endgame +5 "dagger that hits like a greatsword" far above that. It reads as an endgame reward, not a starter weapon.

## DM Notes

The tension is patience versus payoff. The blade is deliberately weak early — a +1 two-hander is a poor prize for a high-level party — and only becomes extraordinary after four killing blows under one owner. Reaching the gold dagger represents a long investment, and the reversion clause means that investment is fragile: lose it, gift it, or die, and the next bearer starts at bronze. Do not let players "bank" a near-complete cycle by handing it around.

Adjudicate "killing blow" as the strike that drops a living, meaningful foe to 0 hp or below — not the killing of summoned trash, undead destroyed by clerics, or coup-de-grace on the helpless, unless you want the cycle to trivialise. The adamantine-save property is the quiet workhorse benefit; remember the magnetic-force exception, which is the only handle an enemy has against it mid-cycle.

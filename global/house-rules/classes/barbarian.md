# Barbarian (Homebrew Class)

## Description

The barbarian is a warrior from outside the city wall: a tribesman from the cold steppes, a hill clansman, a swamp-dweller, a wanderer of the deep forest. They are not "uncivilised" in the sense of being stupid — most are shrewd, observant, and unsentimental — but they are uncivilised in the sense of not trusting the civilised. To the barbarian, walls keep predators in as readily as they keep them out, written law is a cage built of paper, and a wizard is a man who has decided to make a deal with something he does not understand.

What the barbarian *does* trust is the body, the weather, the steel in their hand, and the fury that rises in them when threatened. This last is the class's signature. A barbarian in rage is harder to kill, hits harder, runs longer, and feels less. A barbarian out of rage is a hunter, a tracker, a survivor — at home in places that would kill a knight in a week.

Most barbarians take to adventuring because the village burned, the herd died, the clan was broken, or the omens drove them out. Some come for revenge. A few come because the world the elders described turned out to be smaller than the world actually is, and they wanted to see for themselves.

## Class Requirements

| Ability | Minimum |
|---|---|
| Strength | 15 |
| Constitution | 15 |
| Dexterity | 12 |

Prime requisites: **Strength and Constitution**. A barbarian with 16+ in both gains the standard 10% XP bonus.

**Allowed alignments.** Any non-Lawful. The class's whole posture — distrust of written law, of cities, of long-range planning by people who have never bled — rules out lawful alignments. Most barbarians are Neutral or Chaotic of some flavour. Good barbarians defend their people; evil barbarians prey on others'; neutral barbarians do both, depending on who fed them last.

**Allowed races.** Human, half-elf, half-orc, dwarf (mountain or hill clans only), centaur (homebrew), lizardfolk (homebrew), minotaur (homebrew), tabaxi (homebrew). Elves and gnomes do not produce barbarians — the temperament is wrong. Halflings, by tradition, do not either, though a half-orc raised by halflings might.

## Mechanics

| Stat | Value |
|---|---|
| Hit Die | d12 |
| THAC0 progression | As fighter (20 at 1st, 19 at 2nd, then +1 per level, capped at 6) |
| Saving throws | As fighter |
| XP table | As ranger (slower than fighter — the d12 and rage are paid for here) |
| Maximum level | Unlimited for humans; demihumans by race (dwarf 9, half-elf 8, half-orc 9, centaur/lizardfolk/minotaur/tabaxi as DM determines per the homebrew race file) |

**Allowed weapons.** Any non-exotic melee weapon, plus shortbow, longbow, javelin, sling, hand axe, throwing axe, spear. **No crossbows** (the barbarian considers them dishonourable cranking machines). **No firearms** if the campaign uses them.

**Allowed armour.** Padded, leather, studded leather, hide, ring mail, scale mail. **No plate of any kind, no chain mail, no banded mail.** A barbarian in heavier armour cannot enter rage, loses the speed bonus, and loses the wilderness skills (the body knows it is wearing a coffin and behaves accordingly). Small or medium shields permitted; no large shields.

**Weapon proficiencies.** 4 at 1st, +1 every 3 levels. Non-proficiency penalty −2 (the barbarian is a born fighter; unfamiliar weapons hurt less than they should). Weapon specialisation permitted as fighter.

**Non-weapon proficiencies.** 3 at 1st level (one of which must be a wilderness skill — Hunting, Tracking, Survival, or Animal Lore), +1 every 3 levels. Suggested: Endurance, Direction Sense, Weather Sense, Rope Use, Running, Intimidation, Set Snares.

## Core Mechanic: Rage

Rage is the heart of the class. A barbarian in rage is something other than what they are at rest — bigger somehow, louder, less afraid, harder to bring down.

**Uses per day:** 1 at 1st level, 2 at 4th, 3 at 8th, 4 at 12th, 5 at 16th.

**Entering rage** takes one round (the round is spent shouting, breathing, working up — no other action that round). The barbarian may also enter rage **involuntarily** when they take damage that brings them below half hp, when an ally drops, or when they fail a save vs. fear: the DM rolls morale for the *barbarian* (rating 14); on failure, rage triggers and is spent.

**While raging:**
- **+2 to hit, +2 to damage** with melee weapons.
- **+2 temporary hp per barbarian level**, gained on entry. These are lost first; any left when rage ends evaporate.
- **+2 bonus to saves vs. fear, charm, and hold effects.**
- **−2 to AC** (the barbarian leaves themselves open).
- **Cannot cast spells** (n/a for pure barbarian, but matters for multi-class). **Cannot use any magic item that requires concentration, command words, or fine motor work** — a magic sword still works, a wand does not.
- **Cannot retreat from melee** while a valid enemy is in reach. Cannot perform finesse actions: lockpicking, careful searching, complex social manoeuvres. Reaction rolls during rage are at −4.
- **Lasts** 3 rounds + 1 per CON point above 14 (a CON 18 barbarian rages for 7 rounds). The barbarian may end rage voluntarily at any time on their turn.

**After rage:** **fatigued** for 1 turn. While fatigued: −2 to hit, −2 to damage, −2 to AC, no running, no second rage. Fatigue from rage does not stack with other fatigue but is not removed by short rest — only by 1 turn of complete inactivity or by magical relief.

**Berserker rage (optional, declared at 1st level — no take-backs).** A barbarian may choose, at character creation, the *berserker* path. Berserkers gain **+4 to hit and +4 damage** (instead of +2/+2) and **+3 hp per level** (instead of +2), but during rage cannot distinguish friend from foe: each round, they must attack the nearest visible living creature, ally or enemy, with the closest one chosen randomly if there are multiple. Allies of a berserker quickly learn not to be the closest body when the rage hits. After berserker rage, fatigue lasts 1 hour, not 1 turn.

The berserker path is not "more powerful" — it is more dangerous to the party. Many tables ban it; many tables love it. Decide at session zero.

## Wilderness Skills

The barbarian comes pre-equipped with the body's knowledge of the wild. These work only outdoors or in natural underground (caves, not built dungeons), and only when the barbarian is in light armour or less.

| Skill | 1st level | Improvement |
|---|---|---|
| Climb (natural surfaces) | 80% | +1% per level after 1st |
| Move Silently (wilderness) | 30% | +5% per level to 90% max |
| Hide in Natural Terrain | 25% | +5% per level to 90% max |
| Detect Noise | 25% | +5% per 2 levels |
| Track | base 30%, +5% per level after 1st (cap 95%) | applies to outdoor and dungeon |

**Wilderness movement.** The barbarian in light armour or less moves **+30 ft per round** in surface combat, and travels overland at +50% speed in any natural terrain.

**Surprise.** In a natural environment, the barbarian surprises on 1–4 on 1d10 (instead of 1–3) and is surprised only on a 1.

**Leap.** Standing long jump = barbarian level + STR damage bonus, in feet (minimum 4 ft). Running long jump doubles that. Standing high jump = half the long jump distance, vertical. The barbarian level 5+ may "land running" — no momentum lost on a successful jump.

## Hardiness

- **+1 per 3 levels to saves vs. poison and disease** (rounded up, so +1 from 1st level).
- **+1 per 4 levels to all saves vs. magic** (rounded down, so first bonus at 4th). This is *separate* from the rage bonus and stacks with it.
- **Cannot be surprised by environmental cold, heat, or weather** — the barbarian was in worse last winter. Saves vs. environmental exposure are at +4.
- **Heals naturally at 2× normal rate** during full rest (not partial rest, not adventuring days). Magical healing is unchanged.

## Distrust of Magic

The barbarian's relationship with magic is the class's defining cultural feature. Mechanically:

- **Will not initiate use of magic items**. A barbarian will not put on an unidentified ring, drink an unidentified potion, or wave an unidentified wand. This is roleplay, not a hard rule — players who want to push past it should explain *why their barbarian, this barbarian, would*.
- **Will use weapons and armour with magical properties** without difficulty: a +1 axe is just an axe that hits harder, and the barbarian has no theological problem with a sharper axe. The same goes for *cloaks of protection*, *boots of striding*, and other passive enhancement items.
- **Reluctant with command-word and charged items.** Wands, staves, scrolls, rods. The barbarian will hand these to a partymate. Forced to use one (e.g. last-resort survival), they take a −2 penalty on the activation and on any to-hit/damage rolls for 1 turn after — the act unsettles them.
- **Will not knowingly accept a *geas*, a *quest*, a *charm*, or any magical compulsion as a tool**, even from an ally. A barbarian charmed by an enemy and then released will, on average, try to kill the charmer at next opportunity. This is a roleplay note, not a forced behaviour.
- **−1 reaction in arcane settings** (mage's tower, magical academy, court wizard's chambers). The barbarian is visibly uncomfortable; the locals notice.

**This is not a power; it is a constraint.** It costs the party utility. The benefit is woven into Hardiness above (the magical save bonus) — the barbarian's body and instincts genuinely resist enchantment.

## Tribal Recognition (1st level)

The barbarian belongs to a tribe, clan, or tradition, named at character creation. The DM and player together establish:

- **The tribe's territory** (one region of the campaign world, by name).
- **The tribe's reputation** (feared, respected, ignored, exiled).
- **The tribe's traditions** (one combat tradition — e.g. greataxe, javelin-and-sling, paired hand-axes — and one ritual practice — e.g. funerary scarring, naming ceremony, oath of the wolf).

Mechanically:
- Within the tribe's territory, the barbarian gets **+2 reaction** with their own tribe and **+2 to all wilderness skill rolls**.
- Other members of the tribe will, by default, offer hospitality (food, shelter for one night) without payment.
- A barbarian exiled from their tribe (a starting-condition option) loses the territory bonus but retains all other class features.

The tribe is a roleplay anchor and a free questline source — the barbarian's tribe has problems the wider campaign can solve, and vice versa.

## Back Sense (5th level)

From 5th level, the barbarian cannot be backstabbed by a thief or assassin operating below their barbarian level — the body senses the approach and turns. Higher-level thieves still get the bonus, but at half the multiplier.

## Reckless Charge (7th level)

Once per encounter, a 7th-level barbarian may make a reckless charge: move up to 4× normal rate in a straight line, attack at the end with **+4 to hit and +4 damage**, but accept **−4 AC for the round and a fall** if the attack misses (knocked prone, lose next action). A reckless charge cannot be made in heavy underbrush, on stairs, or on sloped ground.

## Sundering Blow (9th level)

The 9th-level barbarian, while raging, may declare a sundering blow against an opponent's weapon, shield, or non-magical armour. Resolve the attack normally; on hit, instead of damage, the targeted item makes a save vs. crushing blow at −2. Failure: the item is broken (weapon snaps, shield splits, armour gapes open and counts as one category lighter for the rest of the encounter). Magical items save at their full bonus; artefacts cannot be sundered this way.

## Endurance (every level)

Each level, the barbarian's CON is treated as **1 higher** for purposes of forced-march checks, holding-breath rolls, swimming for distance, and resisting starvation/thirst — capped at CON 25 effective. This is a *quiet* bonus that pays off in long expeditions.

## Stronghold (9th level)

A barbarian may, at 9th level, build a stronghold: a clanhouse, a longhall, a fortified camp. It costs roughly half what a fighter's keep would (the barbarian's followers do most of the labour). The barbarian attracts followers — 4d10 1st-level warriors of compatible race and tribe — who serve as a war-band, not as feudal vassals: they expect to fight alongside the barbarian, share spoils, and be led personally. They will not garrison a building while their leader is elsewhere.

## Restrictions and Drawbacks

- **No literacy at 1st level**. The barbarian cannot read or write any language at character creation, even one they speak. Literacy may be acquired in play — a non-weapon proficiency slot in Reading/Writing — but it takes deliberate work, not a free background.
- **Reaction Penalty in Civilised Settings.** −1 reaction in towns, −2 in cities, −3 in royal courts or temples of lawful deities, until the barbarian has been "domesticated" enough that locals stop staring.
- **No multi-class.** The barbarian's discipline is incompatible with the wizard's books, the priest's vows, the thief's caution. A barbarian is a barbarian. (Some tables permit barbarian/cleric for tribal-shaman builds; this is a session-zero decision.)
- **No dual-class** in the human sense either. Once a barbarian, always a barbarian.
- **Magic-item restrictions** as above — not a rule penalty, but a real constraint on party utility.
- **Cannot maintain rage in heavy armour** — heavier than ring or scale shuts the rage down. The body refuses.
- **Aging** as fighter; the barbarian's hard life often shortens the human span by 5–10 years compared to a settled human's expectancy. (Roleplay flavour; no mechanic.)

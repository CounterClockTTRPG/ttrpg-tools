# Pyramids of the Crossing (Homebrew Item)

## Description

A matched pair of hand-sized stone pyramids, each cut from the same block of pale grey granite. The four faces of each are smooth and unmarked except for a single glyph carved into the base — identical on both, worn slightly as though rubbed often. They are heavier than they look. When held, they are faintly warm.

Each pyramid is inert until its command word is spoken. When it is, the speaker and every creature within 10 feet of the speaking pyramid vanish and reappear at the location of the other pyramid — crowded around it as they were crowded around the first. The speaking pyramid travels with them. The pyramid they arrived at does not move.

The original owners used them to be in two places at once, then in the same place at once, depending on what the situation called for.

## Mechanics

- **Appearance.** Matched pair; visually identical. *Detect Magic* reveals a conjuration/teleportation aura. *Identify* names the property and the command word.
- **Activation.** The holder of a pyramid speaks the command word as an action. The pyramid does not need to be visible to others or held aloft — it just needs to be held by the speaker.
- **Effect.** All creatures (willing or not, conscious or not) within 10 feet of the activated pyramid at the moment of activation are teleported to the location of the *other* pyramid. They arrive in the same relative positions they occupied around the activated pyramid, centred on the destination pyramid. The activated pyramid travels with them; it arrives at the destination alongside its passengers.
- **The destination pyramid does not move.** After the crossing, both pyramids are at the same location. To use the ability again, one pyramid must be physically carried to a new location.
- **Unwilling creatures.** Creatures that do not wish to be transported may make a saving throw vs. spell to resist. On a success, they remain; on a failure, they cross. Unconscious or incapacitated creatures receive no save. Objects and creatures owned by or carried by a transported creature travel with them automatically.
- **Arrival.** Arrival is precise. If the destination space is occupied by a solid object, transported creatures are shunted to the nearest open space — roll 1d8 for direction per creature displaced, landing 5 feet out. If no open space exists within 20 feet, the crossing fails entirely and the activation is wasted (the pyramid does not travel).
- **Range.** Any distance, same plane. Across planes: the command word produces no effect and the use is not consumed (the pyramid simply does nothing).
- **Uses.** Each pyramid has 3 charges. Both pyramids draw from the *same pool* — activating either one expends a charge. Charges replenish at dawn, 1 per day. At 0 charges the pyramids are inert but retain their properties; they simply cannot be activated until charges return.
- **Both pyramids together.** Activating a pyramid when both are within 10 feet of each other is valid but pointless — the destination and origin are the same location. Creatures teleport and arrive exactly where they were. Experienced users learn to check.
- **Detection.** The crossing produces a brief flash of pale light at the origin point (not at the destination). Anyone present sees it; it cannot be suppressed.

## Rarity and Value

Rare. Worth 1,200–2,000 gp as a matched pair. Each pyramid alone is worth a fraction of that — roughly 200 gp as a curiosity, since the cross-charge mechanic means a single pyramid with 3 charges and nowhere to go is of limited use.

## DM Notes

The tactical design is: one twin goes ahead and leaves a pyramid, the other stays back with the party. When needed, the second twin activates their pyramid and the whole group crosses to join the first. Or the first twin is in trouble and calls the others across. The 3-charge shared pool creates real resource decisions — the players can't cross freely and must choose when the moment is worth a charge.

The "both pyramids together" edge case will come up. It is not a bug; it just does nothing. Worth adjudicating calmly rather than adding a rule to prevent it.

For ambushes: the flash at the origin point means the party cannot use the pyramids for a silent extraction. They can vanish, but whoever was watching the origin point saw them go. The destination arrival is silent, which is the asymmetry the party should learn to exploit — arrive quietly, leave visibly.

The unwilling creature save creates interesting hostage/extraction possibilities. Consider how intelligent enemies might exploit this if they acquire one half of the pair.

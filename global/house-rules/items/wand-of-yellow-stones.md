# Wand of Yellow Stones (Homebrew Item)

## Description

A length of gnarled dark-brown wood, irregular and knotted, set with five small yellow stones along its length. The grain runs around and between the gems so naturally that it is impossible to tell whether the wood was carved to hold them or whether stone and wood grew together as parts of one living plant. The stones catch the light dully, like old amber, and warm faintly when a spell sleeps within them.

It is, in essence, a portable reliquary for magic — a way to carry five prepared spells ready to loose in an instant. Its makers, whoever they were, left no mark and no instruction, which is precisely the wand's chief hazard: it remembers what it holds, but it will not tell you.

## Mechanics

- **Appearance.** Dark gnarled wood set with five yellow stones. *Detect Magic* reveals an aura on the wand and on any stone currently holding a spell; *identify* or *legend lore* names the storing property but does not reveal which spells are presently stored.
- **Storing.** Each of the five stones can hold one spell. A caster stores a spell into an empty stone as they would inscribe a scroll or charge a similar item (DM's discretion on time and cost). A stored spell is held at the level and effectiveness of the caster who placed it there.
- **Invoking.** Loosing a stored spell takes a single segment (effectively instantaneous within the round). The spell takes effect exactly as cast by its original storer — same level, range, duration, and save.
- **The blind draw.** The wand does not grant its holder any knowledge of what is stored. To invoke a chosen spell, the user must *already know* it is in there. A user who triggers the wand without knowing what it holds releases a random stored spell instead — DM rolls among the currently loaded stones.
- **Limitations.** Once a stone's spell is loosed, that stone is empty until recharged. The wand confers no spellcasting ability of its own and cannot store a spell beyond the storer's own capacity to cast. An empty wand triggered for a "random" spell simply does nothing.

## Rarity and Value

Uncommon. Worth 4,000–8,000 gp to any spellcaster who wants emergency spells on a one-segment trigger — bodyguards, scouts, and anyone who expects to be caught flat-footed. The blind-draw flaw keeps the price honest; an item that might fling a random spell when you most need a specific one is worth less than a clean wand of charges.

## DM Notes

The interesting feature is the blind draw, which makes the wand a record-keeping object. Reward the player who writes down what they store and in which stone; let the player who didn't suffer the random discharge in full. A found wand should usually arrive *loaded with unknown spells*, so the party's first use is a gamble — identify the stored spells and the gamble disappears, which is itself a worthwhile reward for the spell.

For adjudicating the random trigger, roll evenly among only the stones that currently hold a spell. Where the original is silent on storing time and cost, treat it like scroll-scribing so that loading five stones is a real investment of time and money, not a free action between fights.
